# User Archetypes Library

Reusable archetypes across project types. Always extend rather than duplicate — if a project has a variation, note it in the brief and add it here after the project if it's likely to recur.

---

## Marketing / Landing Pages

### The Skeptical Evaluator
**Who they are:** Arrived via referral, ad, or search. Has seen many similar products. Reads critically, looks for reasons to leave as much as reasons to stay.
**What they want:** To quickly determine if this is worth their time.
**What they know:** The category well. They can spot vague claims immediately.
**What they fear:** Wasting time on something that doesn't deliver. Being sold to without substance.
**Trust signals:** Specificity over superlatives. Real numbers. Named social proof. Transparent pricing or clear next step.
**Design implication:** Lead with the most specific, credible claim you can make. Don't bury the lede. Social proof must be concrete.

---

### The Referred Visitor
**Who they are:** Sent by someone they trust. Already has partial buy-in. Less skeptical but still needs to confirm the referral was right.
**What they want:** Validation that the referral was warranted. A clear path to the same outcome their referrer got.
**What they know:** Probably knows the referrer's use case but not necessarily theirs.
**What they fear:** That this won't apply to their specific situation.
**Trust signals:** Recognition of their specific context. Testimonials that mirror their situation.
**Design implication:** Segment messaging if possible. At minimum, cover multiple use cases visibly.

---

### The Comparison Shopper
**Who they are:** Mid-funnel. Already knows the category, actively evaluating options. May have a shortlist.
**What they want:** Clear differentiators. To understand what makes this the right choice for them specifically.
**What they know:** Competitors well. Will notice if you avoid naming them.
**What they fear:** Making the wrong choice and having to switch later.
**Trust signals:** Direct comparison to alternatives. Honest about tradeoffs. Strong guarantee or low commitment entry point.
**Design implication:** Don't hide pricing or make differentiation vague. Make the comparison easy to do on your page or they'll do it elsewhere.

## Portfolio / Personal Sites

### The Evaluating Peer
**Who they are:** Another professional in the same or adjacent field. Evaluating capability and taste quickly. Has high standards and can spot shortcuts.
**What they want:** To assess whether this person is at or above their level. To see genuine thinking, not just polished output.
**What they know:** The field well. They read between the lines — what's shown and what's left out.
**What they fear:** Wasting time on someone junior presenting themselves as senior.
**Trust signals:** Process transparency. Specificity about role and decisions made. Evidence of hard problems solved.
**Design implication:** Show the work behind the work. Case studies over galleries. Be specific about your contribution.

---

### The Potential Client
**Who they are:** Has a problem, looking for someone to solve it. Less interested in craft details, more interested in fit and confidence.
**What they want:** To feel confident this person can handle their specific project. To understand how to engage.
**What they know:** Their problem well. Design or dev process less so.
**What they fear:** Hiring someone who doesn't understand their context, or who's too junior/senior for their budget.
**Trust signals:** Relevant past work. Clear articulation of the engagement process. Testimonials from clients, not peers.
**Design implication:** Lead with outcomes and client types, not tools or techniques. Make the CTA and next step completely clear.

---

### The Recruiter / Hiring Manager
**Who they are:** Scanning many portfolios quickly. Has a role and is pattern-matching against it.
**What they want:** Signal/noise ratio. Fast answers to: relevant experience? right level? culture fit signals?
**What they know:** The role requirements well. The actual work less so.
**What they fear:** Wasting time going deep on someone who's a clear mismatch.
**Trust signals:** Clear role history. Company names or project scales they recognize. A concise summary that does the filtering work for them.
**Design implication:** Make it scannable first, deep second. Summary and titles matter enormously. Don't make them hunt for seniority signals.

## Web Apps / Dashboards

### The First-Time User
**Who they are:** Just signed up. High motivation, low familiarity. Will form lasting mental models in the first session.
**What they want:** To quickly understand what this does and accomplish one meaningful thing.
**What they know:** The problem they're trying to solve. The interface, nothing.
**What they fear:** Getting lost, making a mistake they can't undo, not seeing value fast enough to justify continuing.
**Trust signals:** Clear empty states. Obvious first action. Forgiving error handling. Progress indicators.
**Design implication:** Onboarding is part of the product, not a tour. Design for the first meaningful action, not feature completeness.

---

### The Power User
**Who they are:** Uses the product daily or near-daily. Has internalized the mental model. Values speed and control over guidance.
**What they want:** To accomplish tasks as efficiently as possible. Shortcuts, bulk actions, customization.
**What they know:** The product deeply. May know it better than most of the team.
**What they fear:** UI changes that break their muscle memory. Features hidden behind discoverability friction.
**Trust signals:** Keyboard shortcuts. Density options. Predictable behavior. Changelog transparency.
**Design implication:** Don't optimize onboarding at the expense of power use. Progressive disclosure should reveal, not hide.

---

### The Infrequent Returning User
**Who they are:** Uses the product occasionally — weekly, monthly, or seasonally. Loses familiarity between sessions.
**What they want:** To re-orient quickly and accomplish a specific task without re-learning the whole product.
**What they know:** The product conceptually but not the details. Gets confused by things they've done before.
**What they fear:** Not being able to find what they need. Feeling like they've forgotten something obvious.
**Trust signals:** Consistent layout. Good empty states and recency surfaces. Clear labeling over clever naming.
**Design implication:** Treat returning users as semi-new. Surface recent activity and likely next actions. Don't assume recall.

---

### The Admin / Manager
**Who they are:** Oversees others' use of the product. Less hands-on with core features, more focused on configuration, reporting, and oversight.
**What they want:** Visibility into what's happening, control over settings, ability to manage users.
**What they know:** The organization's needs. The product's features less directly.
**What they fear:** Loss of control, compliance risk, not being able to answer questions from their team or leadership.
**Trust signals:** Audit logs. Clear permissions model. Export/reporting capabilities. Reliable notifications.
**Design implication:** Admin flows are a distinct UX surface — don't treat them as an afterthought. They often have higher stakes than core user flows.
